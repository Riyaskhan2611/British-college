EID TRADITIONS

In the past, men would either buy a new Kandoora (a long, usually white, robe traditionally warn by Gulf Arab men), or dye one of their existing kandoora’s using walnut oil and cardamom. Women would dress in special eid clothes and prepare their hair with special perfumes and braids. In the past and today, women and girls will apply henna to their hands, while children receive new clothes and small gifts.

EID AL-ADHA

This holiday, whose name is translated as “festival of the slaughter”, comes at the end of the annual Haj pilgrimage to Mecca and celebrates the faith of the prophet Ibrahim. Traditionally, sheep, goats or cows are slaughtered, often with a group of neighbours or members of an extended family buying one animal whose meat is then shared. The meat also is distributed to family, friends and the needy in the communit.

EID AL-FITR

Eid al-Fitr celebrates the end of the Holy Month of Ramadan, during which Muslims fast throughout the day, from before sunrise until sunset. Emirati housewives prepare for Eid al-Fitr by cleaning and arranging the house. In the past, on the first day of the eid, Bedouin men would shoot their guns in the air in an expression of joy. In the past, and also today, men perform popular traditional dances, including Al Razfah..
